# Laytime Fundamentals

## What Is Laytime?

Laytime is the period of time contractually agreed in a voyage charter party during which the shipowner places the vessel at the charterer's disposal for loading or discharging operations, without additional payment beyond freight. It represents the charterer's right to occupy the vessel for cargo work within the budgeted time.

If the charterer uses more time than the agreed laytime, the excess time is called **demurrage**, for which the charterer pays the shipowner a daily (or pro-rata) rate agreed in the charter party. If the charterer completes operations before laytime expires, the saved time is called **despatch**, and the shipowner usually pays the charterer half the demurrage rate for the time saved (unless otherwise agreed).

## The Laytime Calculation Framework

Laytime calculation follows a sequential logic:

1. **Has the vessel become an arrived ship?** — The vessel must reach the agreed contractual destination (port, berth, or anchorage as specified) before the NOR can validly be tendered.
2. **Has a valid NOR been tendered?** — The Notice of Readiness must meet all requirements.
3. **When does NOR become effective?** — Depends on WIBON, WICCON, WIFPON, and acceptance clauses.
4. **When does laytime commence?** — Apply any turn time or notice period.
5. **What time counts as laytime?** — Apply exceptions: SHEX, SHINC, WWD, specific clause exclusions.
6. **When does laytime expire?** — When allowed laytime is consumed.
7. **What is owed?** — Demurrage or despatch.

## Demurrage

Demurrage is a pre-agreed rate of liquidated damages payable by the charterer to the shipowner for the detention of the vessel beyond the agreed laytime. Once laytime is exhausted and demurrage commences, the principle **"once on demurrage, always on demurrage"** applies: time continues to count against the charterer even during periods that would otherwise be excepted (e.g., Sundays under SHEX), unless the charter party expressly provides otherwise.

The demurrage rate is typically expressed as a dollar-per-day figure (e.g., USD 15,000/day pro-rata). It is not compensation for actual loss — it is an agreed fixed amount regardless of what the actual cost of delay was.

## Despatch

Despatch is the mirror of demurrage: a reward paid by the shipowner to the charterer when cargo operations are completed before laytime expires. The typical rate is half the demurrage rate ("despatch at half demurrage"). Some charter parties exclude despatch entirely; others provide for "all time saved" vs "working time saved." The distinction matters:

- **All time saved**: despatch calculated on all laytime remaining, including SHEX periods.
- **Working time saved**: despatch only on the time that would have counted as laytime.

## Port Charter Parties vs Berth Charter Parties

The type of charter party determines when NOR can be tendered:

- **Berth charter party**: The vessel is an arrived ship only when it is **at the berth** nominated by the charterer. NOR cannot be given at anchorage. If the berth is congested and the vessel must wait, the waiting time is at the shipowner's risk (unless WIBON modifies this).
- **Port charter party**: The vessel is an arrived ship when it arrives **within the legal, fiscal, or administrative area of the port** and is at the immediate and effective disposition of the charterer. NOR can be tendered at anchorage, and the charterer bears the risk of congestion.

## Reversible vs Non-Reversible Laytime

When a charter party covers multiple ports:
- **Non-reversible**: Each port has its own separate laytime allocation. Savings at one port cannot offset overruns at another.
- **Reversible**: Loading and discharging laytime is pooled. Time saved loading can offset time lost discharging and vice versa, reducing the overall demurrage exposure.

## Allowed Laytime Formats

Laytime can be expressed in multiple ways:
- A fixed number of days: "10 weather working days"
- A rate-based calculation: "1,000 metric tonnes per weather working day"
- In hours: "72 hours SHINC"
- Separately for loading and discharging ports

When expressed as a rate, the allowed laytime is calculated as: **Total cargo quantity ÷ Rate = Allowed laytime in days (or hours)**.
