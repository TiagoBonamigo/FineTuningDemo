# Laytime Clauses and Time Exceptions

## Time-Counting Regimes

The charter party specifies which periods of time count as laytime and which are excluded. The two most fundamental choices are:

### SHINC — Sundays and Holidays Included
All calendar time counts as laytime, including Sundays and public holidays. Time runs continuously, 24 hours a day, 7 days a week, unless a specific exception applies (such as weather).

### SHEX — Sundays and Holidays Excepted
Sundays and public holidays are excluded from laytime, regardless of whether cargo operations actually took place during those periods. SHEX comes in two variants:

- **SHEX EIU (Even If Used)**: Sundays and holidays are excluded from laytime, *but if cargo operations actually take place on those days*, the time used is counted. If the charterer works on a Sunday, that time counts against laytime even though the clause otherwise excepts it. EIU means the exclusion is suspended when the exception day is actually worked.
- **SHEX UU (Unless Used)**: Sundays and holidays are *unconditionally* excluded. Even if cargo operations take place on a Sunday or holiday, that time does not count as laytime. The charterer effectively gets a free day even if they work the vessel. UU is more favorable to the charterer.

When SHEX is stated without EIU or UU qualification, the interpretation can be disputed; EIU tends to be implied in English law, but this is jurisdiction-dependent. It is best practice to always specify EIU or UU.

### ATDNSHINC — All Time Day and Night, Sundays and Holidays Included
Equivalent to SHINC but explicitly emphasizing that night hours are also included. Used to remove any ambiguity that "working hours" might be implied. Laytime runs continuously around the clock.

### WWD — Weather Working Day
A "working day" is a day on which work can ordinarily be done according to the custom of the port. A "weather working day" is a working day or part thereof during which the weather permits loading or discharging to take place. Time lost solely due to adverse weather does not count as laytime.

Key principles of WWD:
- Time stops only when weather **actually prevents** cargo operations.
- If operations are continuing despite rain or swell, time does not stop.
- If operations stop for a reason unrelated to weather (e.g., gang break), the weather clause is not invoked.
- Partial-day weather stoppages are prorated: if weather stops 6 hours of a 24-hour day, only 18 hours count.
- The burden of proving weather prevention lies with the charterer.

### WWDSHEX — Weather Working Day, Sundays and Holidays Excepted
Combines WWD and SHEX. Both weather exceptions and S&H exceptions apply. This is one of the most common laytime regimes in tramp shipping.

## Notice Periods and Turn Time

After the NOR becomes effective, a **notice period** (or "turn time") is typically provided before laytime commences. This gives the charterer time to mobilize gangs, prepare cargo, or arrange equipment. Common formulations:

- **"Laytime to commence 12 hours after tendering of valid NOR"**: Turn time of 12 hours regardless of when NOR is tendered.
- **"Laytime to commence 6 hours after NOR accepted during office hours, or at 08:00 the next working day whichever first occurs"**: Combines a grace period with an office-hours restriction.
- **"24 running hours after NOR"**: 24 hours clock time, not subject to SHEX or other exceptions during the notice period itself.

The notice period itself is usually not subject to the SHEX or WWD exceptions, but charter party language must be checked carefully. Some charter parties explicitly state whether Sundays fall within the notice period or not.

## Inward Passage

When a vessel arrives at a port anchorage and eventually moves to the berth ("inward passage" or "shifting"), the time taken to move from anchorage to berth may be treated in different ways:

- **Time not to count**: The default in many charter parties — shifting time is excluded from laytime.
- **Time to count as laytime**: Some charter parties include inward passage time as laytime.
- **Time to count at half rate**: A compromise provision sometimes seen in bulk carrier trades.

"Inward passage" begins when the vessel weighs anchor and ends when the vessel is fast at the berth and operations can commence.

## Commencement of Laytime: Interaction of Clauses

When calculating when laytime actually commences, the following sequence must be applied:

1. **NOR tendered** — recorded time in the SOF.
2. **NOR effectiveness** — check WIBON/WICCON/WIFPON; if applicable, NOR is immediately effective. If berth charter party without WIBON, NOR is only effective when in berth.
3. **Notice period starts** — the turn time begins running from when NOR is effective.
4. **Office hours restriction** — if clause states "during office hours", and NOR was tendered outside office hours, the notice period may not begin until the start of next office hours.
5. **SHEX restriction on commencement** — if the notice period expires on a Sunday or holiday under a SHEX regime, laytime may not commence until Monday 08:00 (or the start of the next working period).
6. **Laytime commences** — the first moment at which all conditions are satisfied.

## Working Hours and Night Clauses

Some charter parties contain additional restrictions:

- **"Office hours"**: Typically 08:00–17:00 Monday to Friday, but port custom can vary.
- **"Day shift only"**: Operations restricted to daytime hours; nighttime excluded from laytime.
- **"Daylight hours"**: Common in some grain and fertilizer trades.
- **"In regular working hours"**: Laytime only counts during the port's customary working shift hours.

## Exceptions Clauses (Beyond Weather)

In addition to weather and holidays, charter parties often contain other suspension provisions:

- **Strike clause**: Time lost due to strikes (of dock workers, tugmen, pilots) is excluded — typically at charterers' risk for strikes affecting their side, and at shipowners' risk for strikes of the crew.
- **Breakdown of ship's equipment**: Time lost due to failure of the vessel's cranes, pumps, etc. does not count as laytime (it is the shipowner's risk). However, breakdown of shore equipment is the charterer's risk.
- **Force majeure**: Catch-all exclusion for exceptional circumstances; must be expressly included and narrowly construed.

## The Critical Distinction: Whose Risk Is It?

When any interruption occurs, the key question is whether the cause of the delay falls within the **charterer's risk** (laytime counts) or the **shipowner's risk** (laytime does not count):

- Charterer's risk: shortage of gangs/labor, equipment breakdown on shore side, failure to provide cargo, congestion in the port.
- Shipowner's risk: vessel breakdown, breakdown of ship's gear, failure to maintain vessel in ready condition.
- Shared or disputed: bad weather (governed by the WWD clause), strikes (depends on whose employees), contamination of holds.
